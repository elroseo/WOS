# Orwell's Rules for Writing

A reference for my voice. From George Orwell's 1946 essay "Politics and the English Language." These are general principles to keep writing clear and honest, not absolute laws (Orwell himself said to break them rather than write something barbarous).

Source: [Orwell's Five Rules for Writing (WriteAtHome)](https://www.writeathome.com/blog/2024/1/27/orwells-five-rules-for-writing) · full essay: "Politics and the English Language" (1946).

## The five rules

1. **Never use a metaphor, simile, or figure of speech you are used to seeing in print.** Avoid clichés. They are the crutch of lazy writing. If you want an image, make a fresh one.
2. **Never use a long word where a short one will do.** A long word is not better just because it is long. Use the longer one only when it adds real colour, cadence, or meaning.
3. **If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.** Lean toward brevity, but keep rhythm and tone in mind. "Can be cut" does not always mean "should be cut."
4. **Never use the passive where you can use the active.** Active verbs give writing clarity and energy. Passive voice has legitimate uses, just keep them rare.
5. **Never use a foreign phrase, a scientific word, or jargon if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.** Reach for plain English first. (Common exceptions: etc., i.e., e.g.)

> **The sixth rule (the qualifier):** Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

## How this maps to my voice

These reinforce what is already in [[Writing Style]]:

- Rules 2, 3, and 5 back my **concise, plain-language, no-filler** preferences.
- Rule 4 (active voice) supports **leading with the point** and sounding **confident** (a GitHub brand attribute).
- Rule 1 (no tired clichés) pairs with avoiding **corporate speak** ("synergy", "circle back", "leverage").
- The sixth rule is the reminder: clarity and natural voice win over rigidly applying any rule.
